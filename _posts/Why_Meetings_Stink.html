Anyone who has ever worked with me knows that I dislike lots of meetings. There are two primary reasons I dislike lots of meetings, the first is just the way my brain works and the second is an indication of a much bigger issue. I'm not unique in my dislike of meetings. And the fact is I don't dislike all meetings.

I dislike meetings that:

  1. Are not necessary.
  2. Are not part of a normal process.
  3. A replacement for someone doing their job.
  4. Unproductive.
  5. Invite everyone "Just in case".
  6. Most importantly, miss-managed.

During my career I have seen frequent cases of all of the above. In my experience, controlling the number of meetings and amount of time spent in meetings is the easiest way to reduce engineering overhead.

Meetings cost money and take away from an engineers primary job.

First, when I walk by a conference room full of people my brain will instantly (almost) count the number of people in the room and multiply that by the time they have been there and an average hourly rate for my employees. Effectively I calculate what that meeting is costing while I'm walking by. They next question that follows is "I wonder if that meeting is really worth X dollars".

A quick example, let's say I walk by and see 8 of my engineers sitting in a room with product management. They have been there roughly an hour and my average hourly rate (fully loaded) is 70.00. That meeting is costing 560.00/hr. On top of that cost I have also lost an hour of productivity! (assuming this meeting is not part of the normal development process)

Meetings are not a replacement for process.

Second, if I see this occurring frequently I can only assume there is a hole in the process somewhere. With a well oiled process there is no good reason for that many developers to be meeting with product management frequently.

What too do when you smell a 'stink'?

Like anything you need to develop metrics to determine if there is a real issue. There are a couple of ways you can tackle this issue. Honestly, I recommend both as they are both important metrics for an engineering team.

Engineers need to track time

I know this is 'un-popular' and I intend to write another blog about the importance of time tracking in the future. Put simply, engineers need to track time spent on "planned" engineering work. This is relatively simple to do with most tools available today. Using this information you can calculate your engineering overhead. Overhead being all time spent on un-planned or activities un-related too engineering (e-mail, chat, meetings, etc…). If your overhead is 20% or less that's probably all you need to know. Of course if this changes over time you need to understand why.

Look at your teams calendars

As a manager I have access to all of my direct/in-direct reports calendars (you should too). A weekly or monthly review is another indicator of if there is a problem or not. You could go so far as developing a script which calculates your teams total time spent in meetings (by department) if you like. Honestly this is the most accurate way too determine how much time your team (individually and collectively) is spending with whom.

Setting standards

It's important to understand that before corrective action can be taken some standards need to be set and agreed upon. The most important (in this particular case) being overhead. How much overhead is the company comfortable with? I've experienced anywhere from 10-40% depending on the situation. In general I will shoot for 20%. That equates to 8 hours a week. Many of my old bosses would complain that this is too high.

If you assume that the average engineer spends 1 hour a day managing e-mail, chat, commenting on issues, etc… that leaves 3 hours for meetings. In actuality 20% overhead is pretty hard to hit so if you are near it you are doing great!
